Bug bounty is getting paid to find and report vulnerabilities in real systems —
legally, under a program's rules. It's the closest thing to professional offense
you can do from your laptop.

## How it works

1. A company publishes a **program** with a scope, rules, and reward table.
2. You test only what's in scope, following the rules.
3. You report a valid bug with clear reproduction steps and impact.
4. If it's accepted, you get paid (a *bounty*) and/or reputation points.

Platforms like HackerOne, Bugcrowd, Intigriti, and YesWeHack host programs;
some companies run their own.

## Read the scope first

The scope is the contract. Before sending a single request, know:

- **In-scope assets** — exact domains, apps, IP ranges, mobile apps.
- **Out-of-scope** — what's off-limits (and often what bugs won't be paid).
- **Rules** — rate limits, no social engineering, no DoS, test accounts only.

<Callout type="warning" title="Out of scope means off-limits">
  Testing assets that aren't in scope isn't bug hunting — it's unauthorized
  access. Stay strictly inside the program's defined boundaries.
</Callout>

## Mindset

Volume of low-quality reports gets you nowhere. Pick a target, learn it deeply,
and chase *impact*. The workflow that follows —
[recon](/bug-bounties/recon) then hunting specific
[bug classes](/finding-vulnerabilities/methodology) — is how consistent hunters
operate.
